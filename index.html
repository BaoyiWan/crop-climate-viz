05 — Key Insight

What We Learned

Crop type shapes climate response: Iowa's corn drives the most dramatic seasonal NDVI swing, while Texas cotton endures extreme summer heat with modest vegetation. Precipitation is the wild card — Kansas receives more rain in growing season than Iowa, yet Iowa out-greens them all.

These differences aren't just about geography — they reflect how different crops interact with local climate systems, and how farming practices shape the land surface itself.

06 — Project Notes

Prototype Writeup

What have we done so far?

We extracted monthly MODIS satellite data (NDVI, LST Day/Night) and CHIRPS precipitation data for Iowa, Kansas, and Texas using Google Earth Engine. We built a D3.js interactive webpage with four visualization sections: a US map highlighting the three states, choropleth color maps with a month slider, multi-line seasonal trend charts with variable switching, and scatterplots showing correlations between climate and vegetation variables. The GitHub Pages site is live and all interactions are functional.

What will be the most challenging part to design?

The most challenging aspect will be designing the choropleth maps at the county or sub-state level, which requires additional spatial data beyond the current state-level averages. Accurately mapping spatial variation within each state — showing, for instance, which parts of Iowa are greenest in July — demands raster-to-vector aggregation at finer resolution. Balancing visual clarity across three side-by-side maps while keeping the time slider intuitive will require careful layout and color scale decisions.
